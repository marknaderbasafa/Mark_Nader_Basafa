Historian and Philosopher of Science

Mark Nader Basafa

I work on Thomas S. Kuhn, twentieth-century philosophy of science, scientific historicism, and the intellectual formation of The Structure of Scientific Revolutions. My research reconstructs Kuhn’s pre-Structure development through archival evidence, with special attention to Harvard’s philosophical milieu, critical realism, historicism, Cassirer, Fleck, Lovejoy, Koyré, Sarton, and Conant. My recent work combines archival research and systematic philosophy to reconstruct scientific rationality as historically generated, collectively stabilized, symbolically mediated, and professionally transmitted. My current research extends this framework to artificial intelligence through the concepts of digital thought collectives, co-thinking, and constrained rational normativity.

Areas of Research
A study of Kuhn’s Harvard formation, including Sarton, Conant, Lovejoy, Russell, Langer, Frank, Koyré, and the tensions between logical and historicist approaches to science. Archival work on Kuhn’s marginalia and early lectures, focusing on symbolic forms, thought collectives, textbook science, and historically situated normativity.

Thoughts of the Week

June 8-14
June 1-7

  • Reality is the sum of our constraints. This is not a slogan for a new metaphysical realism, but a way of describing what happens in hybrid science. Hacking taught us to look at intervention: what we can manipulate becomes real enough. Giere taught us to look at models: science represents the world partially and perspectivally. But AI-era science adds another layer. Knowledge now emerges from coupled systems of humans, instruments, datasets, algorithms, simulations, and experimental environments. In such systems, reality is not simply “observed” or “represented”; it is encountered as constraint — as the pattern of resistance, stability, failure, and invariance that survives across these interacting layers.

  • Ian Hacking offers a way to move beyond the stale opposition between Kuhnian relativism and scientific realism. Kuhn teaches that scientific reason operates within paradigms; Hacking replies that science is not only a matter of representing the world, but of intervening in it. The experimentalist does not merely ask whether a theory is true, but whether an entity can be made to do work. If electrons can be sprayed, directed, used, and relied upon to produce effects, then they acquire a kind of reality that survives theory change. This suggests a powerful extension of constrained rational normativity: reason is constrained not only by internal coherence or paradigm rules, but by stable practices of manipulation, calibration, and intervention.

  • Ernst Mach’s Popular Scientific Lectures reminds us that science is not only a machine for producing facts; it is also a discipline of intellectual humility. Mach’s central instinct was to resist the temptation to turn useful scientific concepts into hidden metaphysical realities. Theories, models, atoms, space, time, causality — these are not sacred furniture of the universe. They are tools we use to organize experience economically.

  • Mach’s suspicion of absolute space was not just a technical disagreement with Newton. It was a philosophical act of cleansing. He asked science to stop treating useful abstractions as if they were divine furniture. Space, for Mach, should not be imagined as an invisible container standing behind the world. What exists for science are relations: bodies, motions, masses, sensations, measurements.

Representing and Intervening

Ian Hacking. Hacking’s entity realism is a practice-based version of my cross-theoretical calibration. Entities such as electrons gain rational authority not because they are embedded in one final true theory, but because they can be experimentally stabilized, manipulated, and redeployed across shifting theoretical contexts. In this sense, intervention functions as a calibration device: it allows scientific practice to preserve rational continuity without requiring theoretical permanence.
